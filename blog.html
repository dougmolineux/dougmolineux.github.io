Quantum Mechanics and Computing Terminology
May 11th 2021

There is a noticeable divide between the hardware and software of quantum computing. The hardware side has a big learning curve involving quantum mechanics and all the confusing concepts that come along with that.

Some terms that need further investigation (these quotes were all taken from google). Some of these terms aren't related to quantum computing, or even quantum mechanics, but they have still come up while reading documentation and researching.

Causal Laws (Causality) -

In both Einstein's theory of special and general relativity, causality means that an effect cannot occur from a cause that is not in the back (past) light cone of that event. ... Consequently, the relativistic principle of causality says that the cause must precede its effect according to all inertial observers.

Theory of quanta - quanta is just the plural of quantum.

An a priori probability -

A priori probability refers to the likelihood of an event occurring when there is a finite amount of outcomes and each is equally likely to occur. The outcomes in a priori probability are not influenced by the prior outcome.

empirical hypothesis -

An empirical hypothesis, or working hypothesis, comes to life when a theory is being put to the test, using observation and experiment. It's no longer just an idea or notion. It's going through some trial and error and perhaps changing around those independent variables.

Ultraviolet catastrophe -

The ultraviolet catastrophe, also called the Rayleigh–Jeans catastrophe, was the prediction of late 19th century/early 20th century classical physics that an ideal black body at thermal equilibrium will emit radiation in all frequency ranges, emitting more energy as the frequency increases.
Wikipedia Link

Quantum Entanglement -

Quantum entanglement is a physical phenomenon that occurs when a group of particles is generated, interact, or share spatial proximity in a way such that the quantum state of each particle of the group cannot be described independently of the state of the others, including when the particles are separated by a large distance.

Quantum superposition -

The principle of quantum superposition states that if a physical system may be in one of many configurations—arrangements of particles or fields—then the most general state is a combination of all of these possibilities, where the amount in each configuration is specified by a complex number.

Observables - Dispatching to Store from within a Subscribe
March 13, 2018

I came across this problem today. I attempted to fix a unit test that was testing a service. The service had a function that accepted a parameter, did some processing and then dispatched a change to a redux store.

First, the test got data from the store. Then, it made a call to the service file to change it. Then it selected the changed object from the store, and "expected" that it would change. The project had recently been updated from NgRx 2 to NgRx 4, and so a few changes had occurred. Particularly, the select method now returned an Observable. Previously, the test could just inspect an array, compare values before and after the change.

So I thought I could subscribe to the observable, inspect the value, dispatch the change, and then inspect the value again. However, I came across some interesting behavior, the test would just hang and fail, with no output. What was happening? After rethinking what I had implemented, I realized I was dispatching within a subscribe, and thus creating an infinite loop. The subscribe would trigger after each dispatch (and thus never end). What I wanted was for the subscription to complete after one emission, and I came across the first method. This method seemingly completes after 1 emission.

http://reactivex.io/documentation/operators/first.html

This made it so that after the first subscription, and the first dispatch, we didn't re-enter the subscribe method again.
